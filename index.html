Gravity Furniture — Materials

Sustainable Luxury: How We Source Our Premium Woods

Every plank of wood that enters our workshop carries a story — of the forest it came from, the hands that felled it responsibly, and the months it spent drying before a craftsman ever touched it with a tool.

New: Ethiopian Olive Wood Collection

We are proud to announce our newest material partnership — a small cooperative of ethical foresters in southern Ethiopia who harvest olive wood from trees that have naturally completed their fruit-bearing lifecycle. The result is an extraordinarily dense, richly figured timber unlike anything we have worked with before.

"Ethiopian olive wood has grain patterns so unique that no two pieces of furniture we make from it will ever look alike."

Our Sourcing Standards

All timbers used by Gravity Furniture are FSC-certified or sourced through verified ethical partnerships. We conduct annual audits of every supplier and publish our full supply chain report each spring. Our walnut comes from managed forests in the US Midwest, our teak from a 40-year-old plantation in Myanmar, and our oak from Scandinavian community forests.

Gravity Furniture — Design Insight

5 Interior Design Trends Defining 2026

From global design fairs to our own client conversations, five clear movements have emerged this year — and Gravity Furniture is already responding to each one with new pieces and finishes.

01 — Warm Minimalism

The cold, stark minimalism of the last decade is giving way to something warmer: natural textures, earthy palettes, and fewer but more considered pieces. Our new Terra dining range — in oiled oak and hand-thrown ceramic — is a direct response to this shift.

02 — Sculptural Seating

Chairs and sofas are becoming art objects. Clients are asking for pieces that draw the eye even when unoccupied. Our Forme lounge chair, with its organically carved solid oak shell, has been our most requested piece of 2026.

"Clients no longer want a room full of furniture. They want three or four exceptional pieces that define the space completely."

03 — Integrated Lighting, 04 — Biophilic Design & 05 — Heirloom Thinking

Built-in brass lighting, living moss walls paired with furniture, and the return of pieces designed to be passed down through generations — these three trends point toward the same conclusion: people want furniture that means something, not just something that looks good in a photograph.
